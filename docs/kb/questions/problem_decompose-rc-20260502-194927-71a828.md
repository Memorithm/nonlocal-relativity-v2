# Stage 02: Problem Decompose

```markdown
## Source
The sub-questions are derived from the problem statement for **Claw‑AI‑Lab‑Local**, a local autonomous research tool powered by a quantised 7B LLM. The decomposition addresses the core challenge: achieving GPT‑4o‑level autonomous research performance on a single GPU through system‑level innovation (iterative refinement with execution feedback and self‑critique) rather than model scale.

## Sub-questions

1. **SQ1 – Refinement‑loop architecture:**  
   *What is the optimal design of an iterative refinement loop (plan → execute → observe → debug → revise) that enables a quantised 7B model to reliably generate, debug, and improve deep‑learning experiments and their write‑ups within a 6‑hour compute budget?*  
   This includes the choice of feedback signals (error traces, metric deltas, reviewer mock‑scores), the structure of self‑critique prompts, and the trade‑off between loop depth and latency.

2. **SQ2 – Task‑agnostic tool integration:**  
   *How can a small LLM be instrumented to effectively use a limited set of research tools (code execution sandbox, result parser, LaTeX compiler, literature search stub) without fine‑tuning, and what memory/context management strategies prevent catastrophic forgetting or context overflow across multiple loop iterations?*  
   This addresses the agent’s ability to maintain coherent state over long, multi‑step trajectories.

3. **SQ3 – Performance parity with GPT‑4o baseline:**  
   *Under the given hardware and time constraints, can the local agent achieve Automated Reviewer scores (novelty ≥ 2.5, validity ≥ 2.0) on at least 3 of the 5 AI Scientist tasks, and what is the statistical evidence for non‑inferiority compared to the published GPT‑4o results?*  
   This is the primary empirical validation sub‑question.

4. **SQ4 – Failure mode characterisation:**  
   *What are the dominant failure modes of the local agent (e.g., repetitive loops, hallucinated results, incomplete papers, excessive runtime) and how do they differ from those of frontier‑model agents, as revealed by a systematic error taxonomy on the benchmark tasks?*  
   This provides diagnostic insight for future improvements and clarifies the limits of small‑model autonomy.

5. **SQ5 – Cost‑efficiency and reproducibility boundary:**  
   *What is the actual electricity cost per paper and the end‑to‑end reproducibility rate (i.e., fraction of runs that produce a valid paper without human intervention) when the system is executed on the target hardware, and how sensitive are these metrics to prompt variations and quantisation formats?*  
   This addresses the secondary success criteria and practical deployment concerns.

## Priority Ranking
The sub-questions are ranked by their criticality in meeting the primary success criterion and the overall project feasibility:

1. **SQ1 – Refinement‑loop architecture** (Highest)  
   Without a working loop that compensates for the model’s limited reasoning, the agent cannot produce any valid output; this is the core system contribution.

2. **SQ3 – Performance parity with GPT‑4o baseline**  
   Directly measures the project’s main claim. If parity cannot be demonstrated, the project fails its primary objective.

3. **SQ2 – Task‑agnostic tool integration**  
   Enables the loop to interact with the necessary environment; a failure here would block any experiment execution, making SQ1 and SQ3 impossible to assess.

4. **SQ4 – Failure mode characterisation**  
   Essential for interpreting results, guiding debugging, and writing a strong paper, but secondary to achieving a working system and positive results.

5. **SQ5 – Cost‑efficiency and reproducibility boundary**  
   Addresses the secondary success criteria and strengthens the open‑science narrative, but can be evaluated after a functional prototype exists.

## Risks
- **Loop instability:** The refinement loop may amplify errors rather than correct them, leading to degenerate outputs or infinite loops that exhaust the time budget. (Affects SQ1, SQ3)
- **Model hallucination in scientific writing:** The small LLM may fabricate results, citations, or experimental details that are difficult to automatically detect, undermining validity scores and trust. (Affects SQ3, SQ4)
- **Benchmark misalignment:** The AI Scientist tasks, originally designed for GPT‑4o, may contain hidden assumptions (e.g., prompt phrasing, expected output length) that unfairly penalise a smaller model, making the comparison invalid. (Affects SQ3)
- **Hardware variability and time overruns:** The 6‑hour constraint may be breached due to unexpected experiment durations or inefficient LLM inference, causing missed deadlines and incomplete evaluations. (Affects SQ3, SQ5)
- **Reproducibility fragility:** Small changes in quantisation, prompt wording, or environment (CUDA version, library dependencies) could lead to non‑deterministic behaviour, making it hard for others to replicate the reported scores. (Affects SQ5)
```
